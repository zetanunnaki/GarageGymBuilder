## Why a Dip Belt Beats Holding a Dumbbell

Most beginners try to add weight by holding a dumbbell between their feet. This works at light loads but quickly becomes impossible:

- 30 lbs is the practical max for foot-held dumbbells
- The dumbbell drops mid-set if your feet relax
- Shoulder mechanics get distorted by foot tension
- Risk of dropping iron on your toes (genuinely dangerous)

A dip belt solves all of this. The weight hangs from your hips via a chain, leaves your legs free to move naturally, and lets you load up to your full strength capacity.

## Three Exercises Beyond Dips

The name "dip belt" is misleading — this thing does way more than dips:

### 1. Weighted Pull-Ups
The single best exercise for back strength. Add a 25 lb plate, do sets of 5. Add 10 lbs every two weeks. Watch your back grow.

### 2. Weighted Dips
Chest and tricep destroyer. Most underrated upper body exercise. Add 45 lb for sets of 8.

### 3. Belt Squats
Game-changing for people with back issues. Stand on two boxes, hang weight from the belt between your feet, squat down. Loads the legs without spinal compression.

## How Much Weight Should You Add?

A general progression for weighted pull-ups:

- **Bodyweight x 10 reps:** Add 10 lbs
- **+10 lbs x 8 reps:** Add 5 lbs
- **+25 lbs x 5 reps:** Add 5 lbs
- **+45 lbs x 5 reps:** You're stronger than 95% of the population

Same logic for dips. The Iron Bull belt comfortably handles all of these progressions.

## Comfort Tips

The biggest complaint about dip belts is hip discomfort under heavy loads. Three fixes:

1. **Wear thicker shorts** — direct skin contact under 90+ lbs hurts
2. **Position the belt low** — sits on your hips, not your waist
3. **Use a towel as padding** — for ultra-heavy work (200+ lbs)
