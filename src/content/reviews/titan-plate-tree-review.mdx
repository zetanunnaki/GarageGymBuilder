## 6-Month Durability Update

Six months loaded with approximately 500 lbs of Olympic plates across all six pegs. The frame has shown zero sway, tipping, or structural concerns. The powder coating has a few chips where plates have scraped during loading and unloading, but no rust has formed on the exposed steel — even in our unheated garage.

The barbell holders on top are the highlight feature we did not expect to appreciate this much. Having two barbells stored vertically saves a surprising amount of floor and wall space. The barbell slots fit both standard Olympic bars and fat-grip specialty bars without issue.

The one limitation that becomes clear over time: the pegs are slightly short if you stack more than four 45 lb bumper plates on a single peg. Standard iron plates stack higher per peg because they are thinner. Plan your plate distribution across pegs accordingly.

## Compared to Alternatives

**Titan Plate Tree vs DIY Plate Storage:** Some lifters bolt plate storage pegs directly to their power rack or wall. This works and costs less, but it limits how you arrange your gym and keeps heavy weight attached to a fixed location. A freestanding tree lets you position your plates wherever is most convenient and move the whole unit if you reconfigure your space.

**Titan Plate Tree vs Rogue Vertical Plate Tree ($175):** The Rogue version has longer pegs, thicker steel tubing, and a more refined finish. It also costs nearly twice as much. For a home gym with under 600 lbs of plates, the Titan holds everything securely at a much better price. Upgrade to Rogue only if you have 800+ lbs of plates or want matching Rogue aesthetics.
