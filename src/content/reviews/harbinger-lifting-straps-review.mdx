## When to Use Straps (and When Not To)

Lifting straps are not a replacement for grip training. They're a tool to extend your working sets when grip becomes the limiter. General rules:

### Use straps for:
- **Heavy deadlift back-off sets** — save grip for main work
- **High-rep rows** — back fatigue matters more than grip
- **Shrugs** — you'll drop the bar from fatigue long before your traps fail
- **Rack pulls** — heavy partial deadlifts
- **Farmer's walks** (optional, many prefer raw grip)

### Don't use straps for:
- **Your working deadlift sets** — train grip at max loads
- **Pull-ups** — use bodyweight grip only
- **Olympic lifts** — you need to release the bar fast
- **Competition lifts** — illegal in powerlifting meets

## How to Use Them

The first time using straps is frustrating. Here's the process:

1. **Thread the strap through the loop** so it forms a circle around your wrist
2. **Place the strap loose-side-down** against the bar
3. **Wrap the loose end around the bar** toward you
4. **Grip the bar** — the strap should be pulling tight against the knurling
5. **Check both wrists before lifting** — uneven wrap = bar rotation under load

Practice with an empty barbell before trying it with 400+ lbs. Watch a quick YouTube tutorial — it takes 10 minutes to learn and saves years of grip-limited training.

## Figure-8 Straps vs These

Some lifters prefer figure-8 straps for maximum deadlift security:

- **Harbinger (standard wrap):** More versatile, faster to unwrap if you need to drop the bar
- **Figure-8:** More secure for max-effort pulls, harder to escape quickly

For home gym lifters, standard wrap straps like the Harbinger are the right choice. Figure-8s are specialty equipment for max-effort deadlift singles.
