## 3-Month Durability Update

Three months of regular use with bands ranging from 15 to 80 lbs of resistance. The foam grips still feel plush and show no signs of compressing or peeling. The webbing that wraps the band loop is holding up without fraying — this was our biggest concern going in, and TRX clearly over-engineered this connection point.

The carry bag is a nice touch for travel, though we mostly leave the handles clipped to our band set at home. The quick-connect system lets you swap between band resistances in seconds, which is the entire point of this product. Once you train with handles, going back to gripping a raw loop band feels like a downgrade.

## Compared to Alternatives

**TRX Bandit vs Gripping Bands Bare-Handed:** Loop resistance bands work without handles — you just grip the band itself. But the rubber digs into your hands on heavier bands, limits the exercises you can do comfortably, and makes it hard to replicate cable machine angles. The Bandit handles transform a $15 loop band into something that feels like a cable station. For lifters who use bands as a primary training tool, handles are essential.

**TRX Bandit vs Generic Band Handles ($10-15):** Cheap Amazon band handles use thinner webbing, harder plastic grips, and weaker stitching. They work for light bands but start slipping or breaking with heavier resistance. The TRX Bandit is built for heavy bands (up to 150+ lbs of resistance) and feels secure at loads where generic handles feel sketchy.
