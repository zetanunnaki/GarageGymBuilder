## 3-Month Usage Update

After three months of regular use across deadlifts, pull-ups, and barbell rows, we are about halfway through the 8 oz bottle. That is roughly 60 to 70 applications — solid value for $16. The formula consistency has not changed, and the flip-top cap has not leaked in our gym bag.

We noticed that one coat lasts a full training session for moderate sweaters. Heavy sweaters may need a second application mid-workout for deadlift or pull-up intensive sessions. The residue washes off hands with warm water and soap in about 30 seconds. Equipment (barbells, dumbbells, pull-up bars) stays cleaner compared to block chalk — just wipe the bar with a dry towel between sessions.

## Compared to Alternatives

**Liquid Grip vs Block Chalk:** Block chalk is cheaper per application (about $0.05 vs $0.20 for liquid) and provides a slightly grippier feel for max-effort lifts. But it creates a mess — chalk dust on equipment, floors, and clothes. If you share your gym space with family or train in a finished area, liquid chalk is the practical choice. For a dedicated chalk-friendly garage gym, block chalk is fine.

**Liquid Grip vs FrictionLabs Liquid Chalk:** FrictionLabs Secret Stuff ($15-20 for 2.5 oz) is the premium option used by climbers. It lasts longer per application and has a drier feel. But at 3x the cost per ounce, it is hard to justify for general lifting. Liquid Grip gives you the best balance of grip performance and cost per use.
