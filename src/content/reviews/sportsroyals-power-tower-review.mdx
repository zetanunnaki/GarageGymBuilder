## What Sets the Sportsroyals Apart

The budget power tower market has two tiers. Sub-$120 towers like the [Stamina 1690](/reviews/stamina-power-tower-review/) top out at 250 lbs and use thinner tubing. Above $250, you get commercial-grade stations from Body-Solid and XMark with 600+ lb capacities. The Sportsroyals sits in the sweet spot at $149 with a 450 lb rated capacity, thicker steel tubing, and an adjustable backrest — features that usually require spending $50-100 more.

The 450 lb capacity matters more than most people realize. If you weigh 200 lbs and wear a 40 lb weighted vest for dips, you are already at 240 lbs. Add the dynamic force of the dip movement and you are effectively loading the frame at 300+ lbs. A 250 lb-rated tower would be at its limit. The Sportsroyals has meaningful headroom for weighted calisthenics.

## Assembly

Assembly takes 60-90 minutes with basic tools. The hardware is well-organized in labeled bags, and the instructions are better than average for this product category. Two people make the job faster but one person can manage it — the heaviest individual component weighs about 20 lbs.

**Tips from our build:**
1. Assemble on the floor, then stand the tower upright. Do not try to build it standing.
2. Thread all bolts hand-tight first, then torque in sequence. The holes align better when you leave adjustment room.
3. Apply medium-strength threadlocker (blue Loctite) to the main structural bolts. This prevents loosening from vibration during dynamic movements.
4. Level the rubber feet on your specific floor surface before first use. Uneven feet are the number one cause of perceived wobble.

## The Training Experience

### Pull-Ups and Chin-Ups

The pull-up bar sits at approximately 86 inches from the floor (adjustable across several height settings). The bar itself is a straight cylindrical bar with knurled grip sections — no multi-grip handle on this model. The grip diameter is comfortable for medium to large hands. Smaller hands may want to add a pair of foam bar grips for comfort during high-rep sets.

We tested strict pull-ups, chin-ups, wide-grip pull-ups, and neutral-grip (by rotating hands on the bar at an angle). The tower remained stable during strict movements with testers up to 215 lbs. There is a perceptible sway during the concentric phase of each rep, but it does not feel unsafe. We would not recommend kipping or butterfly pull-ups — the lateral force exceeds what the base geometry is designed for, and the tower rocks noticeably.

### Dips

The dip handles are padded with firm foam over steel, angled slightly inward to approximate a natural shoulder position. The spacing is approximately 22 inches center-to-center, which works well for chest-focused dips with a forward lean or tricep-focused dips with an upright torso.

The backrest pad supports you during the mount and dismount. Its angle is adjustable to accommodate different torso lengths. During the actual dip movement, your body should be clear of the backrest — if you are leaning against it during dips, you are doing them wrong.

Dip stability is excellent. The vertical force of dips loads the tower directly downward through the frame, which is its strongest axis. We performed weighted dips with a 45 lb plate on a dip belt (245 lbs total load) and the tower felt solid.

### Leg Raises and Knee Raises

The captain's chair position uses the arm pads and backrest. The arm pads are 3 inches thick with dense foam — comfortable enough for sets of 15-20 reps without elbow pain. The backrest prevents your body from swinging during strict leg raises.

Hanging leg raises from the pull-up bar are also possible, though the bar height limits full extension for taller athletes (over 6'1") unless you bend your knees.

### Push-Up Handles

The base of the tower includes push-up handles at the floor level. These are basic but functional — they elevate your hands about 4 inches off the ground, increasing range of motion at the bottom of the push-up and reducing wrist strain. The handles are positioned slightly wider than shoulder width, targeting the chest and anterior deltoids.

## Exercises You Can Do on the Sportsroyals Power Tower

Here is a complete bodyweight program using only this tower:

### Upper Body Push
1. **Chest dips** — lean forward 30 degrees, wide grip
2. **Tricep dips** — upright torso, elbows close
3. **Elevated push-ups** — hands on base handles, feet on floor
4. **Deficit push-ups** — hands on handles for extra range of motion

### Upper Body Pull
1. **Pull-ups** — overhand grip, shoulder width
2. **Chin-ups** — underhand grip, shoulder width
3. **Wide-grip pull-ups** — hands outside shoulders
4. **Negative pull-ups** — jump to top, lower slowly over 5 seconds (for beginners building toward full pull-ups)

### Core
1. **Hanging knee raises** — from pull-up bar
2. **Captain's chair knee raises** — from arm pads
3. **Hanging leg raises** — straight legs from pull-up bar (advanced)
4. **L-sit hold** — from dip handles (advanced)
5. **Windshield wipers** — from pull-up bar (very advanced)

### Sample Full-Body Circuit (20 Minutes)

1. Pull-ups x 8
2. Dips x 12
3. Hanging knee raises x 15
4. Push-ups (handles) x 15
5. Rest 60 seconds
6. Repeat 4 rounds

This circuit hits chest, back, shoulders, arms, and core. Add a weighted vest to progress over time.

## 4-Month Durability Report

After four months of training 4 days per week, with testers ranging from 155 to 215 lbs:

- **Frame:** Zero structural issues. No cracks, no bending, no weld failures. Bolts remained tight (we checked monthly).
- **Padding:** Arm rest foam compressed approximately 15% from new. Still comfortable but noticeably thinner. Back pad shows similar compression.
- **Powder coat:** Minor scuff marks where plates contact the frame during weighted dips. No rust, no peeling.
- **Rubber feet:** Holding their grip on concrete. No sliding or walking during use.
- **Pull-up bar:** Knurling still intact. The bar itself shows no bending under 215 lb strict pull-ups.

If you are over 180 lbs, plan to do weighted calisthenics, or want better padding and adjustability, the extra $20 for the Sportsroyals is worth it.

## Who Should Buy the Sportsroyals Power Tower

**Buy it if:**
- You want a complete bodyweight training station for under $200
- You plan to progress into weighted dips and pull-ups
- You weigh up to 250 lbs (with room for a weighted vest or dip belt)
- You train with strict form — no kipping

**Skip it if:**
- You want a multi-grip pull-up bar with neutral handles — look at the Body-Solid or XMark power towers ($300+)
- You do CrossFit-style kipping pull-ups regularly — the tower is not stable enough for explosive lateral movement
- You already have a power rack with pull-up bar and dip attachments — a power rack does everything a power tower does, plus barbell work
