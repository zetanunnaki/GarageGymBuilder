## Home Gym Programming: Pick One, Run It

The biggest progression killer in home gym training isn't bad equipment, bad form, or insufficient effort. It's program-hopping. Beginners switch programs every 2 weeks chasing the next thing on YouTube. Advanced lifters chase Russian protocols, conjugate, daily undulating, you name it.

The lifters who actually get strong pick one good program and run it for 16+ weeks. That's the secret.

## Programs by Experience Level

### Beginner (0-1 year of training)

**Starting Strength** or **StrongLifts 5x5**. Both are linear progression programs where you add 5 lbs per session until you stall. Both work.

- 3 days per week
- 3-5 sets of 5 reps on 4-5 compound lifts
- No accessory work needed
- Add weight every session
- Lasts 3-6 months until you stall on every lift

The full beginner template is in our [home gym programming guide](/guides/home-gym-programming-guide/).

### Intermediate (1-3 years of training)

**5/3/1 Boring But Big**, **Texas Method**, or **Greyskull LP**. All three add weekly variation and longer progression cycles.

- 4 days per week
- Heavy day, volume day, light day rotation
- Percentage-based loading (% of 1RM)
- Deload every 4 weeks
- Lasts 6-12 months per cycle

### Advanced (3+ years of training)

**Sheiko**, **Conjugate (Westside)**, **Daily Undulating Periodization**, or a custom program from a coach.

- 4-6 days per week
- High frequency, high volume
- Multiple training waves
- Periodization peaking for meets or PRs

## Programming Principles That Apply at Every Level

Five principles that don't change regardless of experience:

1. **Progressive overload is the only thing that builds strength.** Every program is just a structured way to add weight, reps, sets, or density over time. Without progression, no program works.

2. **Volume drives hypertrophy. Intensity drives strength.** Want bigger? Run more sets per muscle. Want stronger? Run heavier with lower reps. Most people need both.

3. **Recovery is a training variable.** Skipping deloads, sleeping 5 hours, under-eating protein — all of these crash even the best program. Treat recovery as part of the program, not optional.

4. **Track everything.** A workout log is the difference between progress and stagnation. Write down every set, every rep, every weight. Without data, you can't progress.

5. **Specificity matters.** If you want a bigger bench, bench more often. If you want a bigger squat, squat more often. Don't expect deadlift work to drive your bench numbers.

## What to Skip

The most common programming mistakes:

- **Constantly changing programs.** You haven't given any one of them enough time to work.
- **Adding "advanced techniques" too early.** Drop sets, rest-pause, accommodating resistance — none of these matter until you've maxed out basic linear progression.
- **Neglecting accessory work.** The big lifts get the headlines but the accessories build the muscles that stabilize them.
- **Skipping mobility.** 5-10 minutes per session of joint-specific mobility work prevents the injuries that derail everything.
- **Ignoring sleep and protein.** No program works on 5 hours of sleep and 50g of protein per day.
