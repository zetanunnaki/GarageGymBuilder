A lifting platform protects your floor, reduces noise, provides a stable surface, and makes your gym look professional. Best of all, you can build one for under $200 in a single afternoon.

## Why Build a Platform?

1. **Floor protection** — distributes impact force across a wide area
2. **Noise reduction** — plywood + rubber absorbs dropped weight vibration
3. **Stable surface** — no wobble or mat shifting during heavy lifts
4. **Rack anchoring** — bolt your rack to the platform instead of the concrete
5. **Looks professional** — a platform defines your lifting space

## Platform Dimensions

Standard platform: **8' x 8'** (fits a full barbell with plates on both sides)

Compact platform: **8' x 6'** (tight fit but works for single-car garages)

## Step-by-Step Build

### Step 1: Prepare the Base
Lay the first two sheets of plywood side by side on your garage floor. These form the bottom layer. If your platform is 8x8, cut the third sheet in half lengthwise (4x8 becomes two 2x8 pieces) to complete the base.

### Step 2: Glue and Screw the Middle Layer
Apply construction adhesive to the top of the base layer. Lay the second layer of plywood on top, offsetting the seams from the bottom layer for strength. Screw together with 1.5" wood screws every 12 inches.

### Step 3: Cut the Rubber Mats
Cut each 4x6 stall mat to 4x4 (or whatever fills the sides of your platform). The rubber goes on the left and right sides — the drop zones where weight plates contact the platform.

### Step 4: Center Section
The center 4x4 section stays as bare plywood (or add a thin rubber mat). This is where you stand. The firm surface is better for squats and deadlifts than thick rubber.

### Step 5: Attach Rubber Sections
Apply construction adhesive to the plywood and press the cut rubber mats into place on both sides. Let the adhesive cure for 24 hours before heavy use.

### Final Layout
```
[  RUBBER  |  PLYWOOD  |  RUBBER  ]
[  4' x 8' |  4' x 8'  |  4' x 8' ]
```

The center plywood section is where you stand. The rubber sections are where plates land during deadlifts and drops.

## Optional Upgrades

- **Bolt your rack** to the platform for extra stability
- **Stain or seal** the center plywood section for moisture protection
- **Add a logo** with spray paint and a stencil for the gym aesthetic
- **Install rubber edge trim** around the perimeter for a clean look

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using OSB instead of plywood** — OSB delaminates with moisture and impact
2. **Skipping the glue** — screws alone allow layers to shift over time
3. **Making it too thin** — two layers of 3/4" plywood minimum for adequate strength
4. **Not offsetting seams** — aligned seams create a weak point

## Common Platform Mistakes

Beyond the basics already mentioned, here are the mistakes that ruin platforms after weeks or months of use.

### Building Too Small
An 8x6 platform sounds reasonable until you start deadlifting with a full-size barbell. A loaded Olympic bar is over 7 feet long. If your platform is only 6 feet wide, plates hang off the edges and land on bare concrete. Always go 8x8 if your garage allows it.

### Using Indoor-Grade Plywood
Not all plywood is equal. Exterior-grade or marine-grade plywood handles moisture and humidity far better than interior-grade. Garage environments have temperature swings and condensation — interior plywood delaminates within a year. Spend the extra $10-15 per sheet for exterior grade.

### Skipping the Top Rubber Layer
Some builders leave the entire platform as bare plywood to save money. The drop zones absolutely need rubber — without it, every deadlift set hammers plywood directly, and you'll crack through both layers within months. The center standing section can stay as plywood, but the sides need rubber protection.

### Not Leveling the Platform
A platform built on an uneven garage floor wobbles during heavy lifts. Before assembling, check your floor with a 4-foot level. If the floor slopes, use thin plywood shims under the low side of the base layer until the platform is level. Even a half-inch of wobble feels dangerous with 400 lbs on your back.

### Forgetting About Moisture Underneath
Concrete sweats. Moisture trapped between the platform and concrete breeds mold and rots plywood from the bottom up. Place a plastic vapor barrier (a cheap tarp or 6-mil poly sheeting) under your platform before building. This $10 precaution adds years to the platform's life.

### Over-Tightening Screws
Cranking screws too tight into plywood causes the wood to split around the screw head, weakening the joint. Drive screws snug but not beyond — the construction adhesive between layers does most of the structural work. Pre-drill holes if your plywood tends to split.
