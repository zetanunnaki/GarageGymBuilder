Bad lighting kills gym motivation faster than a cold barbell. Most garages come with a single 60-watt bulb that casts shadows everywhere. Proper gym lighting costs under $100 and transforms the training experience.

## Why Lighting Matters

Beyond seeing what you're doing, good lighting has real performance effects:

- **Safety** — You need to clearly see the barbell path, rack holes, and floor obstacles
- **Motivation** — Bright, well-lit spaces feel professional and energizing
- **Video quality** — If you record lifts for form checks, lighting is everything

## The Best Type: LED Shop Lights

4-foot LED shop lights are the gold standard for garage gym lighting. They're bright, energy-efficient, and cost $15-30 each.

### What to Look For

- **Lumens** — Aim for 4,000-5,000 lumens per light. You want 2-4 lights for a standard garage
- **Color temperature** — 5,000K (daylight) is ideal for training. Avoid warm/yellow tones
- **Linkable** — Some LED shop lights chain together so you only need one outlet
- **Mounting** — Flush-mount or hanging chains both work

## Placement Strategy

### Two-Light Setup (Minimum)
Place two 4-foot LED lights parallel to the long axis of your garage, spaced about 6 feet apart. This eliminates the worst shadows.

### Four-Light Setup (Recommended)
Create a rectangle of light centered over your training area. This provides even, shadow-free illumination from every angle.

### Avoid These Mistakes

1. **Don't put lights directly above the bench** — you'll be staring into them during bench press
2. **Don't use a single bright light** — it creates harsh shadows
3. **Don't forget the garage door side** — when the door is down, you lose all natural light

## Lighting Placement Tips

Getting the right lights is half the battle. Placing them correctly is what eliminates shadows and makes your gym safe to train in.

### Map Your Training Zones First
Before mounting anything, identify where you spend the most time: under the rack, at the bench, in the deadlift zone, and at your warm-up area. Each zone needs direct overhead lighting. Sketch your gym layout and mark where lights should go before drilling a single hole.

### Offset Lights From the Bench Press
The most common lighting mistake is mounting a bright LED directly above the bench press position. When you're lying face-up pressing heavy weight, a 5,000-lumen light in your eyes is blinding and dangerous. Offset bench lighting 2-3 feet to either side, or mount lights parallel to the bench rather than perpendicular.

### Height Matters
In a standard 8-foot ceiling garage, flush-mount lights work best. Hanging lights on chains adds style but reduces clearance for overhead press and pull-ups. If your ceiling is 9 feet or higher, hanging chains with 6-12 inches of drop are fine and let you adjust height later.

### Light the Mirror Wall
If you have mirrors for form checks (and you should), position at least one light to illuminate the mirror wall evenly. Side-lighting a mirror creates glare and hot spots. A light mounted 2-3 feet in front of the mirror, angled slightly toward it, gives the clearest reflection.

### Don't Forget the Corners
Garage corners are where equipment storage, cardio machines, and accessories tend to live. A single light strip or plug-in LED puck light in dark corners prevents tripping over plates, bands, or cables. These cost $10-15 and plug into any outlet.

### Natural Light Integration
If your garage has windows or you train with the door partially open, position your artificial lights to complement natural light rather than compete with it. On the garage-door side, add extra artificial lighting so your gym stays well-lit even when the door is closed on cold or rainy days.
