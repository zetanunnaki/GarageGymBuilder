Buying a barbell is confusing. The specs read like gibberish — "190K PSI tensile strength, 28.5mm shaft, Westside knurling, needle bearing sleeves." Let's cut through the jargon and explain what actually matters.

## The 7 Specs That Matter (Explained in Plain English)

### 1. Tensile Strength (PSI)

**What it means:** How much force the bar can take before permanently bending.

**What to buy:**
- **Under 150K PSI:** Avoid. Budget bars will bend under heavy loads.
- **150K-190K PSI:** Mid-range. Fine for most home gym lifters up to 500 lb deadlifts.
- **190K+ PSI:** Premium. Will never bend under any realistic load.

**Rule of thumb:** For most home gym use, 190K PSI is the sweet spot.

### 2. Shaft Diameter

**What it means:** How thick the bar is in the grip area.

**What to buy:**
- **28mm:** Standard for Olympic weightlifting. Slightly whippy.
- **28.5mm:** Best all-purpose diameter. Good for everything.
- **29mm:** Power bars and squat bars. Stiffer, no whip.

**For most home gym lifters, 28.5mm is the best choice.**

### 3. Knurling

**What it means:** The crosshatch pattern cut into the shaft for grip.

**Types:**
- **Passive:** Comfortable but slippery under heavy deadlifts
- **Medium:** Sweet spot for most lifters
- **Aggressive:** Great grip, tears up hands during high-rep sets

**Center knurling** is important for back squats (prevents bar slip). Some bars omit it for Olympic lifters who find it uncomfortable.

### 4. Sleeve Rotation

**What it means:** How freely the sleeves (ends where plates go) spin.

**Types:**
- **Bushings:** Cheaper, adequate for powerlifting (squat/bench/deadlift). Slower spin.
- **Needle bearings:** More expensive, smoother, required for Olympic lifts (cleans, snatches).

**Which to buy:**
- **Powerlifting only:** Bushings are fine (save money)
- **Olympic lifting:** Bearings required

### 5. Whip

**What it means:** How much the bar flexes under load.

**Types:**
- **Stiff bars:** Best for squats and bench. No energy lost to flex.
- **Whippy bars:** Best for Olympic lifts. Flex helps launch the bar.

Most home gym lifters should buy stiff bars. Olympic lifting is the exception.

### 6. Finish

**What it means:** The coating that protects the steel from rust.

| Finish | Grip | Rust Resistance | Maintenance | Cost |
|--------|------|----------------|-------------|------|
| Bare Steel | Best | Lowest | Weekly oil | Cheapest |
| Black Phosphate | Good | Low-Medium | Regular oil | Low |
| Black Oxide | Good | Low | Regular oil | Low |
| Zinc | Decent | Medium | Occasional | Medium |
| Chrome | Slick | High | Minimal | Medium |
| Cerakote | Smooth | High | Minimal | High |
| Stainless | Excellent | Highest | Almost none | Highest |

**For garage gyms:** Black phosphate or zinc is the sweet spot.

### 7. Weight & Length

Standard Olympic barbells:
- **Men's bar:** 20 kg (44 lbs), 7'2" long, 28-29mm shaft
- **Women's bar:** 15 kg (33 lbs), 6'6" long, 25mm shaft
- **Training bar:** 5-10 kg (11-22 lbs), 5'5" long, ~25mm shaft

**Buy a men's 20kg bar unless you specifically need a shorter/lighter bar.**
