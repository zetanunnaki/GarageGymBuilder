A weight bench is the second-most important purchase in your home gym after the power rack. It unlocks bench press, dumbbell exercises, step-ups, and dozens of other movements. But the wrong bench creates frustration and can even cause injury. This guide teaches you exactly what to look for.

## Bench Types: Which One Do You Need?

### Flat Bench
- **What it is:** Simple horizontal bench, fixed position
- **Best for:** Bench press only (squats, dumbbell work)
- **Cost:** $80-200
- **Skip if:** You want incline or decline work

### Adjustable Flat-Incline Bench
- **What it is:** Bench that adjusts from flat to vertical
- **Best for:** Most home gym users (80%+ of needs)
- **Cost:** $100-400
- **Buy this for:** Flat bench press, incline press, shoulder press, rows

### FID Bench (Flat/Incline/Decline)
- **What it is:** Bench that adjusts flat, incline, AND decline
- **Best for:** Lifters who want decline work
- **Cost:** $200-600
- **Worth it if:** You specifically want decline bench press

### Olympic Weight Bench
- **What it is:** Bench with integrated Olympic bar catches
- **Best for:** Lifters without a separate rack
- **Cost:** $200-400
- **Skip if:** You already have a power rack

**For most home gym owners, an adjustable flat-incline bench is the right choice.**

## The 6 Specs That Actually Matter

### 1. Weight Capacity

Don't just add your body weight plus the bar. Factor in dynamic force:

- **500 lbs:** Minimum acceptable for benching
- **700-800 lbs:** Sweet spot for most home gym lifters
- **1,000+ lbs:** Commercial grade, overkill for most users

**Rule of thumb:** Buy a bench rated for 3x your heaviest bench press.

### 2. Pad Width

The bench pad width affects shoulder retraction during bench press:

- **10" wide:** Budget benches. Fine for smaller lifters.
- **11-12" wide:** Standard. Works for most adults.
- **12"+ wide:** Competition width. Best for bench press.

**For most lifters, 10.5-12" is the sweet spot.** Wider benches are more comfortable but cost more.

### 3. Pad Thickness & Firmness

You want firm, dense foam — not soft:

- **Too soft:** Loses stability under heavy loads
- **Medium firm:** Comfortable but stable
- **Rock hard:** Uncomfortable for long sessions

The pad should compress slightly under your weight but not sink more than 1/4 inch.

### 4. Adjustment Positions

More positions = more versatility:

- **3-4 positions:** Budget benches. Works but limited.
- **5-7 positions:** Sweet spot for home gyms.
- **8+ positions:** Premium benches. Finer control.

**Key positions to look for:**
- Flat (0°)
- Low incline (15-30°)
- Moderate incline (45°)
- High incline (60-75°)
- Vertical/seated (90°)
- Decline (optional, -10 to -30°)

### 5. Zero Gap Design

This is the most important feature on premium benches:

**The problem:** Cheap benches have a gap between the seat pad and backrest at incline positions. This gap is uncomfortable and unstable during heavy pressing.

**Zero gap benches:** No gap at any incline position. The seat pad and backrest align perfectly.

**Examples:**
- ❌ Budget benches (FLYBIRD) — visible gap at steep inclines
- ✅ Premium benches — zero gap at all positions

The FLYBIRD works for most home users. The gap is only an issue above 45° incline.

### 6. Stability & Build Quality

Pick the bench up. Does it feel solid?

- **11-14 gauge steel:** Good quality
- **Heavy base:** Less wobble
- **Rubber feet:** Prevents sliding
- **Triangular base design:** More stable than rectangular
