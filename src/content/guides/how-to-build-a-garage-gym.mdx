Building a garage gym is one of the best investments you can make in your fitness. No commute, no waiting for equipment, no monthly fees. But doing it wrong means wasted money and a space you never use.

This guide covers everything from measuring your space to making your first purchase.

## Step 1: Measure Your Space

Before buying anything, measure your garage and map out the available space.

**Minimum requirements:**
- **Power rack footprint**: 4' x 4' minimum, plus 4' in front for barbell clearance
- **Ceiling height**: 8' minimum (9'+ preferred for pull-ups and overhead press)
- **Total floor space**: 100 sq ft minimum for a functional setup

If you have a single-car garage (~200 sq ft), you can build an excellent gym. A two-car garage (~400 sq ft) gives you room for everything.

## Step 2: Install Flooring First

This is non-negotiable. Bare concrete will crack under dropped weights and destroy your equipment. Read our [complete gym flooring guide](/best-gear/best-gym-flooring/) for all the options.

**Our recommendation:** 3/4" horse stall mats from Tractor Supply Co. At ~$50 per 4x6 mat, they're the most cost-effective gym flooring on the planet. Check our [detailed flooring options guide](/guides/garage-gym-flooring-guide/) for full installation instructions.

## Step 4: Climate Control

Garages get hot in summer and cold in winter. Plan for it. Read our [complete ventilation guide](/guides/garage-gym-ventilation-guide/) and [winter training guide](/guides/winter-garage-gym-training/) for the details:

- **Fans**: A 20" box fan or wall-mounted fan is essential for summer
- **Heater**: A portable propane or electric heater for winter sessions
- **Insulation**: If you're serious, insulate the garage door — it makes the biggest difference

## Step 5: Lighting

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Buying cardio first** — A treadmill without a rack is a coat hanger. Get the rack first.
2. **Skipping flooring** — You'll regret it the first time you drop a deadlift.
3. **Overbuying accessories** — Start with compounds. You don't need a calf raise machine.
4. **Ignoring ceiling height** — Measure before you buy a tall rack.
5. **Not budgeting for plates** — The rack is useless without weight on the bar.
