Your garage gym floor takes a beating. Dropped deadlifts, heavy rack loads, and sweat all conspire to destroy bare concrete. The right flooring protects your floor, your equipment, and your joints.

## Why You Need Gym Flooring

Three reasons to invest in proper flooring before anything else:

1. **Protect your concrete** - Dropped weights will crack bare concrete
2. **Reduce noise** - Rubber absorbs impact sound (your neighbors will thank you)
3. **Improve safety** - Non-slip surface prevents accidents during heavy lifts

## The Best Flooring Options

### Horse Stall Mats (Our Top Pick)

The 4x6-foot rubber horse stall mats from Tractor Supply Co. are the gold standard of garage gym flooring. At roughly $50 per mat, they're the most cost-effective solution.

**Best for:** Deadlifts, general lifting areas, and full-gym coverage

### Rubber Tiles (Interlocking)

Interlocking rubber tiles are easier to install and offer a cleaner look. They're ideal if you want a more polished gym aesthetic.

**Best for:** Dedicated workout rooms, aesthetic-focused builds

### Rolled Rubber

Commercial-grade rolled rubber provides seamless coverage and the most professional result. It's also the most expensive option.

**Best for:** Large dedicated spaces, commercial-grade home gyms

## How Much Flooring Do You Need?

A standard two-car garage is roughly 400 sq ft. Here's the cost breakdown:

- **Horse Stall Mats**: ~$350 for full coverage
- **Rubber Tiles**: ~$600-800 for full coverage
- **Rolled Rubber**: ~$800-1,200 for full coverage

## Installation Tips

1. Clean your concrete thoroughly before laying any flooring
2. Let rubber mats off-gas outdoors for 48 hours before installation
3. Use a utility knife and straight edge for precise cuts
4. Leave a small gap at walls for expansion

### Cutting Horse Stall Mats
Horse stall mats are dense and difficult to cut. Use a sharp utility knife with a straight edge (a 4-foot level works well). Score the mat repeatedly rather than trying to cut through in one pass — 4-5 passes is normal. Flip the mat over and finish from the other side. Replace your blade frequently; dull blades cause ragged cuts and wasted effort.

### Dealing With Gaps and Shifting
Heavy mats on smooth concrete will shift over time, creating gaps. Options to prevent this:
- **Double-sided carpet tape** along the seams — cheap and effective
- **Gorilla Glue or construction adhesive** on the concrete — permanent but difficult to remove
- **Let gravity do the work** — push mats tight against a wall on two sides so they can only shift into the wall, not apart

### Handling the Rubber Smell
New rubber mats smell terrible. The off-gassing is strongest during the first 2-4 weeks. Speed up the process by leaving mats outside in direct sunlight for 2-3 days before installation. After installation, keep the garage door open and run a fan for the first week.

## Flooring by Budget

### Under $200: Horse Stall Mats (Partial Coverage)
Buy 3-4 horse stall mats and cover only the lifting area — under the rack and the deadlift zone. Leave the rest as bare concrete. This is the approach most beginners should take.

### $200-500: Horse Stall Mats (Full Coverage)
Cover the entire garage floor with stall mats. At ~$50 per 4x6 mat, a two-car garage needs roughly 7-8 mats. This gives you a clean, uniform look and full protection.

### $500-800: Interlocking Rubber Tiles
Rubber tiles give a more polished gym aesthetic with easier installation. They interlock without adhesive and can be removed if you move. Better for dedicated workout rooms or if appearance matters to you.

### $800-1,500: Rolled Rubber
Commercial-grade rolled rubber provides seamless coverage with no gaps or shifting. It's the professional standard and the last flooring you'll ever buy. Best for large spaces where you want zero maintenance and maximum durability.

### Hybrid Approach (Best Value)
Use horse stall mats for the main lifting area and cheaper foam tiles or bare concrete for storage and cardio zones. This gives you premium protection where it matters without overspending on areas that don't take impact.
